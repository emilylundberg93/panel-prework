Sean is co-founder and CTO of Found AI, an AI infrastructure company built on a simple conviction: the industry has spent its energy on better models and more data, while the real bottleneck, how knowledge is structured, related, and trusted over time, has gone largely unsolved. Found AI tackles that frontier directly, turning a company's raw data into a typed, traceable, permissioned knowledge layer that AI can actually reason over.

Before Found AI, Sean co-founded and led engineering at Mavely and Swyft, where the job was turning messy, unstructured data into measurable outcomes. That's the lens he'll bring to this panel: less about the models, more about the foundation underneath them.

03 / THE THROUGH-LINE

What this panel is really about.

Everyone has more data and better models than they did two years ago, and most still can't get reliable, accountable intelligence out the other end. The gap isn't compute or model quality. It's the foundation underneath. Four themes carry the 40 minutes:

THEME 01

Structure before scale

Why "more data, bigger model" stalls, and what changes when relationships and context are structured first.

THEME 02

Trust as architecture

Provenance, lineage, and permissioning built in by design, not bolted on after.

THEME 03

Own it or rent it

Whether you build and control your knowledge foundation or depend on someone else's, and what each choice costs.

THEME 04

From foundation to action

What separates a foundation that demos well from one that survives production and the next model upgrade.

04 / SHARED GROUND

Questions I may ask everyone.

Opening go-around

  • In your world, when does data actually become intelligence worth acting on? And how far off is that, day to day?
  • What's the most valuable thing your AI surfaces today that you simply couldn't get before the data was structured for it?

Mid-panel

  • Where do you let the model figure things out, and where does the foundation just have to know the answer already?
  • Everyone says "trust." What do you actually mean by it, and what do people keep getting wrong?
  • When you got the structure right, what kind of intelligence becomes available that you didn't expect? New patterns, predictions, connections?
  • Your data and your models are both moving targets. How do you keep the foundation from going stale?
  • What's one thing your industry figured out that the other three should just copy?
